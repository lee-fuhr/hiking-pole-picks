How to choose the right hiking pole

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

For 2026, the best hiking pole for you depends on your terrain, budget, and hiking style. Prioritize adjustability for versatility, cork or foam grips for comfort, and a locking mechanism that you can rely on, aluminum is durable and affordable, while carbon fiber saves weight at a higher cost.

What should I look for in a hiking pole?

The three big factors are material, grip, and locking system. Aluminum poles are tough, budget-friendly, and can bend under pressure rather than snap, but they weigh a bit more. Carbon fiber is lighter and absorbs more vibration from the trail, but it is more expensive and can crack under extreme side loads. For most day-hikers and backpackers, a good aluminum pole offers the best balance of durability and value. Grip material matters a lot for comfort over many miles. Cork grips mold to your hand, wick sweat well, and stay comfortable in hot or cold weather. Foam grips are soft, cheap, and warm to the touch but can get slippery when wet. Rubber grips are durable but tend to make your hands sweat and are best reserved for cold-weather poles with thick gloves. Locking mechanisms come in twist-lock or lever-lock styles. Lever locks are faster to adjust and easier to repair in the field, while twist locks can be more compact but may slip over time if not maintained. Look for a system that feels secure when you lean your weight on it and that you can operate easily with gloves on.

Fixed-length vs. adjustable vs. folding poles: which is right for me?

Adjustable poles are the most versatile option for most hikers. They let you shorten the poles for steep ascents, lengthen them for descents, and collapse them to strap onto your pack. Many adjust from roughly 25 to 55 inches, which covers virtually all body heights and terrain needs. Fixed-length poles are lighter and simpler, with no moving parts to fail. They are a good choice if you know your exact preferred length and mostly hike flatter trails. However, they are less convenient for travel or for sharing between hikers of different heights. Folding poles are the most packable, often collapsing into three or four sections small enough to fit inside a carry-on bag. They are popular among fastpackers and trail runners who need minimal weight and bulk. The trade-off is that they are typically fixed-length once assembled (or have only minor adjustment), and they may not be as rigid as a two-section telescoping pole.

Cork, foam, or rubber: which grip is best?

Cork grips are the favorite among long-distance hikers for a reason. They conform to your hand shape over time, wick moisture away, and do not get sticky or cold. If you hike in hot weather or sweat heavily, cork is likely your best bet. Foam grips are lighter and cheaper than cork. They feel soft and warm initially but can become slick once they get wet from rain or sweat. They also wear down faster than cork. Foam is a solid choice for budget-conscious hikers or those who hike primarily in cool, dry conditions. Rubber grips are the most durable and grippy when dry, but they make your hands sweat quickly in warm weather, and they get very cold in winter. They are best reserved for hiking in cold climates where you will wear thick gloves, because rubber provides a secure hold even with gloves on.

How do I choose the right hiking pole length?

A common rule of thumb is to adjust your poles so that your elbow forms a 90-degree angle when you hold the grips with the tips on the ground. For flat terrain, that usually means the pole length is roughly 68 to 75 percent of your height. But this is just a starting point, your ideal length also depends on the steepness of the trail and your personal comfort. On uphills, shorten the poles by a few inches so you can push off effectively without raising your shoulders. On downhills, lengthen them to take pressure off your knees and improve stability. If you hike on varied terrain, adjustable poles are essential for dialing in the right length on the fly. When you first use poles, start on gentle terrain and experiment with different lengths until you find what feels natural. Pay attention to any strain in your wrists, shoulders, or neck, those are signs the poles are too long or too short.

Do I need shock-absorbing poles?

Shock-absorbing poles include a spring mechanism inside the shaft that cushions impact with each stride. This can reduce jarring in your wrists and elbows on hard-packed trails or long descents. Many hikers find them comfortable for paved sections or very rocky ground. However, the added complexity means more weight, a higher price, and one more part that can break or wear out. The damping effect also saps a bit of your forward energy with each plant, which some experienced hikers dislike. For most backpackers and day-hikers on mixed terrain, a standard rigid pole is simpler, lighter, and more reliable. Reserve shock absorption for trail runners or hikers who spend a lot of time on hard, flat surfaces.

Frequently asked questions

Do I really need hiking poles?

Not strictly, but they take a lot of load off your knees and hips, especially on descents or with a heavy pack. They also improve balance on uneven terrain and give you an upper-body workout. Many hikers find they reduce fatigue significantly on long days.

Are carbon fiber poles worth the extra cost?

If you are counting every ounce and prioritize vibration dampening, yes. A pair of carbon fiber poles can save you several ounces over aluminum. But they cost more and are less impact-resistant. For most hikers, a good aluminum pole offers better value and durability.

Can I use hiking poles for trekking poles interchangeably?

The terms are used interchangeably. What some call trekking poles are the same adjustable poles used for hiking. There is no functional difference. The same poles work for day hikes, backpacking trips, and even Nordic walking with the right baskets.

How do I maintain my hiking poles?

Rinse off mud and salt after each trip, especially around the locking mechanisms. Dry them fully before storing. For twist-lock poles, periodically clean the threads and apply a light grease. Check the tips and replace carbide tips when they become blunt. Tighten any loose screws or bolts.

What is the difference between trekking poles and hiking poles?

There is no difference, the terms are synonyms. Both refer to two poles used to assist with balance and reduce joint impact while walking on trails. Some manufacturers label them as trekking poles and others as hiking poles, but the products are identical.

In shortThe best hiking pole is the one that fits your body, your budget, and the terrain you explore most. Focus on a comfortable grip, a reliable lock, and the right length adjustability. If you can, try a friend’s pair or visit an outdoor store to feel the difference between materials and weights before you buy.
