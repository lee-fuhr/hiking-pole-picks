Hiking Pole questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Hiking poles reduce strain on your knees and improve stability, especially on uneven terrain or when carrying a heavy pack. This guide answers the most common questions buyers ask, from grip materials to locking mechanisms, so you can choose a pair that fits your hiking style and body. The right poles can make hikes feel easier and safer, but only if you understand what to look for.

What are the main benefits of using hiking poles?

Hiking poles transfer some of your upper body weight to your arms, which reduces the load on your knees, hips, and ankles. On descents, this can cut the impact force by up to a quarter, according to several studies. They also improve balance on rocky or slippery trails, and they help you maintain a steady rhythm, especially when combined with a walking technique like Nordic walking. For hikers carrying a heavy pack, poles provide extra stability and can help prevent falls. They also double as emergency shelters (with a tarp) or as a support for a camera mount when needed. The trade-off is that your arms will tire, especially if you are not used to using poles, and they add a small amount of weight to your kit.

How do I choose the right length for my hiking poles?

The most common rule is to choose poles that allow your elbow to bend at roughly a 90-degree angle when you hold the grip on flat ground. For adjustable poles, this means setting the length so that when the tip is on the ground, your forearm is parallel to the trail. A good starting point is to subtract roughly 24 inches from your height in inches, but that is only a rough guide. Most hikers prefer adjustable poles because they can shorten them for steep uphills (to engage upper body muscles) and lengthen them for descents (to absorb impact). Fixed-length poles are lighter and simpler but less versatile. If you hike mostly rolling terrain, a fixed pole around your elbow height works fine, but for varied mountain trails, adjustability is worth the extra complexity.

Cork, foam, or rubber grips – which is best?

Cork grips are the most popular for serious hiking because they mold to your hand over time, provide good moisture wicking, and stay comfortable in hot weather. They are less grippy when wet compared to some foams, but they dry quickly. Foam grips are very comfortable, absorb shock well, and are usually cheaper, but they can get slippery when sweaty or wet and tend to wear out faster. Rubber grips are durable and provide excellent grip in all weather, but they can be too firm and cause blisters on long days. Your choice depends partly on climate and hand sweat. If you hike in dry conditions or have fairly dry hands, cork is a great all-rounder. If you hike in rain often or have very sweaty hands, a textured foam or rubber might work better. Many poles let you swap grips, so you can experiment over time.

What locking mechanisms should I look for?

There are two main types: twist locks and lever locks. Twist locks are simple, lightweight, and less prone to breaking, but they can slip if not tightened sufficiently or if the collar gets corroded. Lever locks (often called flick locks or cam locks) are easier to adjust on the trail and usually hold more securely, but they add a bit of weight and have more parts that can fail. For most hikers, lever locks are the safer bet because you can quickly tighten them with a gloved hand and you can see if they are fully engaged. Twist locks require more initial adjusting and can be finicky in cold or muddy conditions. Many quality poles now use a combination of both, or a reinforced lever design. Whichever you choose, make sure the locking mechanism is replaceable, because they do wear out after a few seasons of heavy use.

How heavy should my hiking poles be?

Weight matters but is often overemphasized. A typical pair of aluminum poles weighs around 1 to 1.5 pounds, while carbon fiber poles can be 10 to 20 percent lighter. Lighter poles reduce arm fatigue on long days, but they are usually more expensive and can be less durable if you abuse them. For day hikes on well-maintained trails, a medium-weight aluminum pole is a great balance of cost, strength, and comfort. For backpacking trips where every ounce counts, ultralight carbon or thin aluminum poles can save you a few ounces, but be prepared to treat them more carefully. Heavy poles (over 1.5 pounds per pair) can feel clunky and tiring, but they are often the most affordable and durable. The best approach is to consider your typical hike length and pack weight: heavier loads and longer days favor lighter poles; short day hikes or budget builds can use heavier ones without much penalty.

How do I use poles effectively on different terrain?

On flat ground, plant the pole opposite your forward foot for a natural walking rhythm. On uphills, shorten the poles slightly (by a few inches) and plant them near your feet to push yourself up. On downhills, lengthen them to absorb impact and plant them ahead of your body to act as brakes. For traversing slopes, keep the uphill pole shorter and the downhill pole longer to keep your torso level. When crossing streams or slippery rocks, use a three-point rule: keep two poles and one foot, or two feet and one pole, planted at all times. On steep, loose terrain, plant the poles firmly before each step and put your weight through your arms. Avoid over-reaching; the pole tip should land close to your foot to avoid loss of balance. Practice on easy trails first, and you will quickly develop a rhythm.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use hiking poles on an airplane?

Yes, but hiking poles are generally not allowed as carry-on items because they can be used as weapons. Pack them in your checked luggage or ship them ahead. Some airlines may allow collapsed poles in a carry-on if the tips are covered, but it is safer to check them.

Are carbon fiber poles worth the extra cost?

Carbon fiber poles are lighter and dampen vibration better than aluminum, making them more comfortable on long, rocky trails. However, they are more expensive and can shatter if caught in a rock crevice. Aluminum poles are tougher and cheaper, but they transfer more shock and can bend permanently. The choice depends on your budget and how much weight you want to save.

Do I need poles for easy, well-groomed trails?

Not strictly, but they can still reduce fatigue and improve your posture, especially on longer walks. Many hikers on flat paths use poles to engage their upper body and maintain a steady pace. If you have knee or back issues, poles are beneficial even on gentle trails.

How do I maintain my hiking poles?

Rinse them with fresh water after hiking in mud, sand, or sea air to prevent corrosion and grit buildup in the locking mechanisms. Dry them thoroughly before storage. For twist locks, occasionally apply a small amount of grease to the threads. For lever locks, check the tension screw if they start slipping. Replace worn carbide tips and baskets as needed to maintain grip.

Can I use just one pole instead of two?

Yes, many hikers use a single pole for balance, especially on gentle terrain or when they want a free hand for a camera or map. However, using two poles provides better support and reduces the load more evenly on your body. A single pole can also create an asymmetric gait if you rely on it heavily, so try both and see what feels natural.

What is the three-point rule for crossing streams?

The three-point rule means that before you move any single point of contact (foot or pole), at least three other contact points are solidly planted. For example, with two poles planted upstream and one foot locked, you move the other foot. This rule prevents falls on slippery rocks and is especially important when crossing fast-moving water.

In shortHiking poles are a simple tool that can transform your hiking experience, but the best pair is the one that fits your body, terrain, and budget. Start with adjustable poles, a comfortable grip material, and a reliable locking system, then fine-tune from there. Take your time to learn proper technique, and your knees and balance will thank you for miles to come.
